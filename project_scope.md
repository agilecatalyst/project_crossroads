That is an incredibly fascinating, high-stakes, and admittedly controversial concept for a game.

To answer your first question directly: Mechanically and conceptually, yes, it would make for a phenomenal, deeply engaging RTS. The sheer asymmetry of the current Russo-Ukrainian war—combining Soviet-era legacy hardware with bleeding-edge drone warfare, crowdfunding, and Western tech—is a perfect fit for the classic Command & Conquer faction dynamic (think Global Defense Initiative vs. Brotherhood of Nod).

However, from a commercial and cultural standpoint, developing a game explicitly named after a massive, ongoing real-world tragedy would be a massive minefield. Most studios would likely mask it under a fictionalized proxy setting (similar to how games like Act of Aggression or Global Conflagration handle modern conflicts) to avoid being viewed as exploitative.

But if we are "sparring in the unknown" and designing the ultimate, raw, hyper-modern C&C clone blueprint, here is how the specs would look to make it a masterpiece.

The Core Gameplay Blueprint
Classic C&C rules apply: fast-paced base building, sidebar construction queues, resource harvesters, and distinct sub-factions/Generals.

The Economy (The "Tiberium" Twist)
Instead of fictional green crystals, the economy relies on two modern lifelines:

Supply Lines / Material (The Physical Economy): Collected by logistics trucks from depots or cargo drops to build heavy armor and infrastructure.

"Crowdfunding" / Budget (The Digital Economy): Generated by capturing signal towers, internet hubs, or PR broadcast stations. This resource dictates your ability to buy off-map strikes and high-tech drone swarms.

Faction 1: The Russian Armed Forces (The "Mass & Art" Faction)
Design Inspiration: Classic C&C Soviet Union mixed with modern Russian doctrinal reality.

Focuses on massive firepower, heavy artillery, and overwhelming numbers, but suffers from slower tech progression and high vulnerability to electronic warfare.

Mechanic – "Doctrinal Artillery": Russian structures and artillery units can pool their firepower. If three or more artillery pieces fire at the same grid, it triggers a "Saturation Bombardment," destroying base structures quickly.

Unique Units:

T-90M "Proryv" Tank: Heavy frontline MBT featuring "Slat Armor" upgrades that block the first incoming anti-tank missile.

TOS-1A Solntsepek: Short-range, devastating thermobaric rocket launcher that clears out infantry entrenched in garrisoned buildings instantly.

The "Meat-Wave" Conscripts: Cheap, rapidly produced infantry squads that can be massed to overwhelm enemy positions, though they lack morale and suppress easily.

Superweapon – Iskander-M Missile Strike: A massive tactical ballistic missile strike that causes heavy kinetic damage and leaves a lingering zone of destruction.

Faction 2: The Ukrainian Armed Forces (The "Asymmetry & Tech" Faction)
Design Inspiration: C&C Allied / Nod hybrid.

Focuses on mobility, concealment, high-tech Western hand-me-downs, and an incredibly lethal decentralized drone network.

Mechanic – "Decentralized Command": UAF structures don't strictly require a central Construction Yard to function. They can deploy hidden "Mobile Command Vans" that extend a stealth construction radius.

Unique Units:

Baba Yaga Hexacopter: A heavy, terrifying night-bomber drone. It moves slowly but can drop high-explosive ordnance that ignores traditional armor.

Bradley IFV (with TOW): Faster and more precise than the Russian counterparts. Excellent at bush-whacking enemy armor from afar.

FPV Kamikaze Drone Teams: Cheap infantry units that act like classic C&C "Terrorists," but steerable from a distance to snipe high-value vehicles.

Superweapon – Neptune / Storm Shadow Salvo: A precision, multi-vector cruise missile strike capable of bypassing automated air defenses if coordinated correctly.

The Missing Link: The 3rd "Wildcard" Faction

The PMC / International Legion Network
A mercenary/volunteer conglomerate that relies heavily on unconventional warfare, fast technicals, corporate black-budget tech, and cyber-warfare. Their gameplay would revolve around sabotaging the economy of the other two players, deploying autonomous AI ground vehicles, and stealing enemy tech.

GPS Jamming: A Russian EW vehicle can create a bubble where the Ukrainian player loses mini-map radar and precision-guided missile accuracy.

Drone Recon: The Ukrainian player can blanket the sky with cheap reconnaissance drones, creating a "perfect information" advantage unless the Russian player deploys specialized anti-drone "electronic rifles" or air defense.

1. Act I: The Opening Scramble (No MCVs, Just Cities)
Instead of deploying an MCV on a blank map, the mission starts with Key Cities already on the battlefield.

The City Grid: Cities act as your primary command structures. They have a fixed "Urban Radius" where you can build defensive lines, barracks, supply depots, and drone manufacturing plants.

The Chaos Mechanic: In the opening mission, you start with three interconnected cities. The EAC (Eurasian Coalition) launches a massive, multi-vector surprise assault. Your goal isn't to wipe them out—it's to hold the urban centers.

The Refugee/Logistics Dilemma: Civil vehicles block the roads. If your military units crush civilian cars to get into position, your Diplomatic Capital Meter drops, hurting your chances of getting Western aid later. You have to use infantry to clear roadblocks and shepherd civilians to safety while fighting off forward armored scouts.

2. The UBR Tech Escalation: Diplomatic Credits & Off-Map Factories
As the campaign progresses into Act II and III, your reliance on external aid becomes a core gameplay mechanic. You can't just build a giant airfield in a warzone and expect it to survive heavy artillery. Instead, you use Off-Map Factories.

The "Safe Haven" Production Queue
Using your Diplomatic Capital, the UBR player can invest in production lines located in friendly neighboring nations (off-map).

These structures cannot be destroyed by the EAC.

You queue up high-tier units (like Western-analogue tanks or heavy airframes) in this off-map queue.

Once built, they are delivered to the map via Strategic Rail Corridors or Secured Air Corridors. If the EAC cuts off the railway lines on the map, your off-map reinforcements can't arrive, forcing you to launch high-stakes counter-attacks to reopen the supply lines.

3. The Air War: "Falcon" Analogues & Deep-Strike Drones
Once you secure your cities and stabilize the front, the UBR unlocks its late-game air capabilities.

The F-16 Analogue: The "Vanguard Falcon" Multi-Role Fighter
Unlike the EAC, which relies on heavy, carpet-bombing Su-34 analogues that require massive forward airfields, the UBR's Falcon operates on a "Strike & Scram" mechanic.

The Air Base: They don't land at a traditional, vulnerable base airfield. Instead, they are stationed off-map.

Gameplay Loop: You call them in via the Sidebar for precise, high-speed strikes. They scream across the map, drop laser-guided munitions on a specific EAC heavy artillery piece or "Satan" missile silo, and immediately punch the afterburners to exit the map before the EAC's automated S-400 anti-air networks can lock onto them.

Custom Long-Range Strategic Drones (The "Tremor" Series)
To mirror the real-world development of custom, long-range one-way attack drones (like the Ukrainian Palianytsia or Lutyi), the UBR gets a dedicated tech branch.

The Assembly Plant: Built inside a secured city hub, this factory pieces together cheap, fiberglass, long-range propeller drones.

The "Waypoint" Mechanic: These drones are slow and fragile, but they have infinite range. The player doesn't just right-click an enemy base; you must physically click and draw a custom flight path on the mini-map, routing the drones around the EAC's radar cones and mobile air defense vehicles.

The Payoff: If you successfully navigate the drone swarm through the gaps in their radar, they slam into the EAC's oil refineries or ammo dumps, causing massive, cascading chain-explosions that cripple the enemy's resource generation for several minutes.

The Strategic Tug-of-War
By combining the Key Cities mechanic with Off-Map Factories, the game becomes beautifully asymmetrical.
